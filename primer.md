# Aflac Incorporated — Strategic Primer

> **Purpose of this document.** Background context for interpreting Aflac earnings calls. This is a curated synthesis of pre-2024 materials (10-Ks, FAB books, investor presentations). For direct, citable claims about current performance, use the 2024–2025 earnings call transcripts. This primer is informed context; the transcripts are the source of record.

---

## 1. Company at a Glance

Aflac Incorporated (NYSE: AFL) is a Columbus, Georgia–headquartered holding company that sells **supplemental health and life insurance**. The company is structured around two operating segments that are essentially two different businesses sharing a corporate roof:

- **Aflac Japan** — by far the larger segment by assets, premium income, and earnings. Sells cancer, medical, nursing care, whole life, and savings-oriented products. Dominant in Japan's "third sector" (supplemental health) market.
- **Aflac U.S.** — sells voluntary worksite products (accident, disability, critical illness, hospital indemnity, life, dental, vision) primarily through career agents and brokers, with growing group/voluntary business.

A small **Corporate and Other** segment houses the parent company, the Bermuda reinsurance entity (Aflac Re Bermuda), and certain investment activities.

**Why this structure matters for PR work.** A statement about "our business" is ambiguous unless you know which segment. Japan and U.S. have very different growth trajectories, margin profiles, distribution challenges, and political/regulatory environments. Management almost always speaks to the segments separately.

---

## 2. The Japan Concentration

Aflac is a U.S.-listed company whose largest business is in Japan. This shapes everything:

- The yen/dollar exchange rate is a constant earnings variable. Management reports financials both including and excluding FX, and "ex-FX" or "currency-neutral" is the metric they emphasize for underlying performance.
- A meaningful share of the investment portfolio is yen-denominated (JGBs, Japanese corporates), with the rest in U.S. dollar–denominated securities held to back the U.S. dollar program inside Japan.
- Regulatory capital is measured under two different regimes: **ESR (Economic Solvency Ratio)** for Japan and **RBC (Risk-Based Capital)** for the U.S. subsidiaries.
- The company runs an "enterprise hedging program" to protect the economic value of Aflac Japan in U.S. dollar terms, including holding ~65% of corporate debt in yen as a natural hedge.

**PR implication.** When the yen weakens, GAAP earnings translated to dollars look worse even when the underlying Japanese business is performing well. Management routinely flags this distinction and the press should too. Conversely, a strengthening yen flatters reported numbers.

## 3. Products

### Aflac Japan product families

- **Cancer insurance** — Aflac's flagship Japan product. Aflac is the largest cancer insurer in Japan.
- **Medical insurance** — supplemental hospital/surgery coverage. Anshin Palette is the current flagship medical product (launched 2025).
- **Income support / disability** — smaller but growing.
- **Nursing care** — for an aging Japanese population.
- **First sector products** — life insurance and savings/annuity products (Whole Life, WAYS, Child Endowment, Tsumitasu). These are technically lower-margin but help with persistency, asset accumulation, and serving the full customer relationship.
- **Tsumitasu** — a savings-oriented "first sector" product launched as a strategic anchor to attract younger customers and cross-sell third sector (cancer, medical) coverage.
- **Miraito** — newer cancer insurance product.

### Aflac U.S. product families

- **Accident** — historically the largest U.S. line by sales.
- **Disability** — short-term disability dominant.
- **Critical illness / cancer / hospital intensive care** — grouped as "Critical Care" in disclosures.
- **Hospital indemnity**
- **Life** — both individual and group.
- **Dental & Vision** — built up via the 2017 acquisition of Argus Dental & Vision; underperformed for several years before management invested in the platform.
- **Group voluntary benefits** — Group Premier Life, Absence Management, and Disability Solutions ("Palette" platform). These are newer growth pillars for Aflac U.S.
- **Consumer Markets** — direct-to-consumer platform.

### What's growing, what's mature

Japan cancer and medical are mature, slow-growth businesses with strong margins. The growth investments in Japan are the first sector products (Tsumitasu) and product refreshes (Miraito, Anshin Palette). In the U.S., the growth story is the group/voluntary platform, dental/vision turnaround, and consumer markets — collectively referred to by management as "buy-to-builds."

## 4. Distribution

### Japan distribution

- **Affiliated and individual corporate agencies** — the historical core. Number declining (8,400+ in 2020 down to ~7,200 by early 2024), but per-agency productivity rising.
- **Banks** — including Japan Post Bank, Dai-ichi Life partnership, and others. Smaller share but stable.
- **Japan Post Group alliance** — long-running strategic partnership for cancer insurance distribution. A milestone for Aflac Japan's reach.

### U.S. distribution

- **Career agents** — Aflac's own salesforce, historically the dominant channel, focused on small to mid-sized employers.
- **Brokers** — growing share, particularly for larger group cases.
- **Average weekly producer count and productivity** are quarterly disclosed metrics. Producer count has been roughly stable in the 6,000–6,600 range; productivity has been climbing.

**PR implication.** Sales force health, agent recruitment, and productivity are recurring themes on calls. Pre-2024, COVID disrupted face-to-face selling badly; recovery has been gradual.

## 5. Key Financial Metrics and What "Good" Looks Like

These are the metrics analysts watch every quarter. Outlook ranges from FAB books and quarterly presentations:

### Aflac Japan (recent ranges, FAB-disclosed)

| Metric | Recent range |
|---|---|
| Benefit ratio | 60% – 66% (target band has shifted lower over time as new business has lower benefit ratios) |
| Expense ratio | 19% – 23% |
| Pretax profit margin | 30% – 38% |

Underlying earned premiums in Japan are running roughly **-1% to -2%** annually as the in-force block matures. Management views this as expected and offset by margin expansion.

### Aflac U.S. (recent ranges)

| Metric | Recent range |
|---|---|
| Benefit ratio | 42% – 52% |
| Expense ratio | 36% – 41% (rising as new growth platforms scale) |
| Pretax profit margin | 17% – 23% |
| Net earned premium growth | 3% – 6% |
| Premium persistency | 78% – 79% |

### Capital and balance sheet

- **ESR with USP (Japan)**: target zone usually >250%, has been running 220%–253%.
- **Combined RBC ratio (U.S.)**: target >600%, has been running 560%–730%.
- **Adjusted leverage**: target range 20%–25%.
- **Holding company unencumbered liquidity**: minimum target $1B; typically running $2–4B.

### Capital deployment

- **Quarterly dividends** are sacrosanct. Aflac is a **Dividend Aristocrat** with 40+ consecutive years of increases.
- **Share repurchase** is the variable lever. Quarterly buybacks have ranged from ~$500M to $1B in recent quarters, with full-year totals of roughly $2.5B–$3.5B.
- Combined returns to shareholders have been roughly $1.0–$1.3B per quarter through 2024–2025.

## 6. Investment Portfolio Overview

The total Aflac investment portfolio is on the order of **$100B+**, dominated by:

- **JGBs and Japanese sovereigns** — large yen book backing Japan policy reserves
- **U.S. corporate bonds** — investment grade, including the "USD Program" inside Japan that adds yield to the yen book
- **Banking/financial institutions, public utilities, other corporates** — diversified
- **Limited but notable commercial real estate exposure** — including transitional real estate loans. CRE has been a recurring source of impairments and credit commentary since 2023.
- **Private equity and other alternatives** — small but notable for "variable net investment income" volatility quarter to quarter.

**PR implication.** When markets question Aflac on credit, the conversation centers on commercial real estate (where impairments have been recurring) and the broader corporate book quality. Management's posture is consistently that distressed CRE valuations don't reflect intrinsic value and they will manage the cycle to maximize recoveries rather than force-sell.

## 7. Reinsurance Strategy

Aflac has built out a reinsurance franchise centered on **Aflac Re Bermuda Ltd.** — a Bermuda-based reinsurer used for both internal capital management and, increasingly, external transactions.

Key milestones to be aware of:
- Internal reinsurance has been used for years to optimize capital between Aflac Japan and Aflac U.S.
- External reinsurance: Q1 2026 marked the first material external transaction — Aflac Re Bermuda assumed a block of whole life annuities from **Japan Post Insurance**. Management characterized the deal itself as immaterial to financials but a strategic milestone for expanding the franchise.
- Management has signaled this is a growth area and a way to deploy capital at attractive returns.

## 8. Recurring Themes Likely to Come Up on Calls

These are subjects you should expect to hear about every quarter or every few quarters:

- **Yen/dollar exchange rate impact on reported earnings** — virtually every call.
- **Lapse and reissue dynamics in Japan cancer** — a recurring conversation since 2023. Older policyholders trade up to newer products; the dynamics affect both reported benefit ratio and the embedded value calculation.
- **Sales momentum in Japan**, especially around major product launches (Tsumitasu, Miraito, Anshin Palette).
- **U.S. group/voluntary growth and dental/vision turnaround** ("buy-to-builds").
- **U.S. agent recruiting and conversion**, especially for the traditional career agent channel.
- **Capital ratios** (ESR, RBC) and any sensitivity commentary.
- **Variable investment income** — the swing factor each quarter, driven by alternatives and PE returns.
- **CRE impairments and loan portfolio quality** — recurring since 2023.
- **Reinsurance** — strategic growth narrative.
- **LDTI assumption unlocks** — typically a third quarter event each year. Affects benefit ratios, can move reported numbers materially.
- **Capital deployment cadence** — dividends and buyback levels.
- **Cybersecurity incident** — the June 2025 incident is now a standing risk-factor disclosure and surfaces in litigation/regulatory commentary.

## 10. The 2020–2023 Strategic Arc (Pre-Transcript Period)

Quick narrative covering the years not directly in the transcript corpus:

### 2020 — Pandemic disruption
- COVID hit both segments hard. Japan sales fell **38%** in 2020 versus 2019. U.S. sales fell **31%**.
- Face-to-face distribution was the bottleneck — sales associates literally couldn't visit small employers (U.S.) or households (Japan).
- Management response: maintain dividend, accelerate digital investments, support agents through the disruption, hold back on aggressive growth investment.
- Capital position remained strong throughout.

### 2021 — Recovery, with caveats
- Japan sales rebounded **9%** off the COVID low; U.S. rebounded **17%**.
- Still well below 2019 levels in absolute terms.
- Management emphasized "duration" — the long-tenure, multi-decade nature of the in-force block being a stabilizer.
- Equity reduction reflected adoption of LDTI accounting standard transition (early adoption by some companies; LDTI applied retrospectively to a January 1, 2021 transition date).

### 2022 — Yen pressure and inflation regime change
- Sharp dollar strength / yen weakness was the dominant macro story. USD/JPY broke through 150 in October 2022, the weakest yen since 1990.
- This compressed reported dollar earnings even as the underlying Japan business held up.
- Inflation and Fed tightening began affecting investment portfolio mark-to-market, though Aflac's long-duration buy-and-hold posture limited operating impact.
- Aflac U.S. began a multi-year persistency improvement story (78%+ range).

### 2023 — LDTI go-live and credit nervousness
- LDTI (ASU 2018-12) became effective January 1, 2023 for SEC filers. This was a fundamental change in how long-duration insurance contracts are accounted for. Aflac restated prior-year financials. Earnings volatility profile changed; comparability with pre-2023 results is limited.
- SVB collapse (March 2023) and the regional banking crisis put a spotlight on insurance investment portfolios. Aflac's exposure was modest, but the questions were intense.
- Commercial real estate stress emerged as a recurring theme — impairments on CRE loans and real estate owned became standing line items in the CFO commentary.
- Dividend Aristocrat streak continued; share repurchase remained robust.

## 11. Useful Glossary (Japan-specific terms)

| Term | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Third sector** | Supplemental health insurance category in Japan (cancer, medical, nursing care). Where Aflac dominates. Distinct from first sector (life/savings) and second sector (P&C). |
| **First sector** | Traditional life insurance and savings products in Japan. Aflac's WAYS, Tsumitasu, Whole Life, Child Endowment products live here. |
| **Tsumitasu** | Aflac Japan's savings-oriented first sector product. Strategic for attracting younger customers and cross-selling third sector. |
| **Miraito** | Newer Aflac Japan cancer insurance product. |
| **Anshin Palette** | New Aflac Japan medical insurance product (launched 2025). |
| **WAYS** | Aflac Japan's variable life-with-savings product line. |
| **Child Endowment** | Japanese savings product structured around a child's education milestones. |
| **JPY/USD or USD/JPY** | Yen/dollar exchange rate. Higher USD/JPY = weaker yen = headwind to reported dollar earnings. |
| **TTM (in FX context)** | Telegraphic Transfer Middle rate — published by MUFG Bank — Aflac's source for the average yen/dollar rate it uses. |
| **ESR** | Economic Solvency Ratio — Japan's regulatory capital framework. Target >250% with USP. |
| **USP** | Undertaking-Specific Parameter — an internal-model adjustment to ESR. Adds points to the ratio. |
| **RBC** | Risk-Based Capital — U.S. regulatory capital framework. Aflac's combined RBC across four U.S. subsidiaries targets >600%. |
| **LDTI** | Long-Duration Targeted Improvements — ASU 2018-12, the GAAP accounting standard that took effect January 1, 2023. Fundamentally changed how insurance liabilities and DAC are measured. |
| **DAC** | Deferred Acquisition Costs — capitalized policy origination costs amortized over the contract life. |
| **DPL** | Deferred Profit Liability — for limited-pay contracts; reclassified under LDTI. |
| **Net premium ratio (NPR)** | Under LDTI, the ratio that determines how much of premium goes to benefits. Recalculated annually. |
| **Assumption unlock** | Annual review of long-term LDTI assumptions, typically in Q3. Can move benefit ratios materially. |
| **Persistency** | Percentage of premiums that renew rather than lapse. Aflac U.S. runs ~78–79%; Aflac Japan runs ~92–93%. |
| **Lapse and reissue** | Phenomenon where existing Japanese cancer policyholders surrender old policies and buy new ones, generating reserve releases. Recurring topic since 2023. |
| **Benefit ratio** | Benefits paid (and reserved) divided by net earned premium. Lower = more profitable. |
| **Variable investment income** | Income from alternatives, private equity, and similar — the volatile component of net investment income each quarter. |
| **Aflac Re Bermuda** | Aflac's Bermuda reinsurance entity. Used for internal capital optimization and, increasingly, external reinsurance transactions. |
| **Buy-to-builds** | Management's term for U.S. growth platforms acquired or built since the late 2010s — Group Life/Absence/Disability ("Palette"), Dental & Vision, Consumer Markets. |
| **Net earned premium** | Premium income recognized as earned in the period, after reinsurance ceded. |
| **Adjusted earnings / Adjusted EPS** | Aflac's preferred non-GAAP earnings metric, which strips out net investment gains/losses, hedge impacts, and other items management considers non-operating. |
| **Adjusted EPS ex-FX** | Adjusted EPS recalculated using prior-year FX rates, isolating operating performance from currency translation. |

## 12. How to Use This Primer

For PR-lens analysis of any earnings call statement:

1. **Anchor the statement to a segment.** Japan or U.S. — they behave differently and have different stakeholders.
2. **Check the macro backdrop.** Use the inflection-points file (companion document) to find the market context for that quarter.
3. **Compare to the recurring themes list.** If it's a topic management talks about every quarter, the story line is well-established. If it's new, it's potentially newsworthy.
4. **Listen for the verbs.** "Solid," "strong," and "in line with expectations" are baseline. Departures from this register are signal.
5. **Distinguish reported from underlying.** Especially for Japan, separate FX-driven optics from operating reality.

# Aflac — Market Inflection Points & Macro Context (2020–2026)

> **How to use this file.** Each entry covers a moment where the macro/industry backdrop meaningfully shaped how Aflac's management was talking about the business. When reading an Aflac earnings call from one of these periods, this is the world the management team was speaking *into*. ## Inflection 1 — Q1 2020 / COVID Onset (call: late April / early May 2020)

### Macro backdrop
- COVID-19 declared a pandemic by the WHO on March 11, 2020.
- U.S. equity markets crashed roughly 34% from peak to trough (Feb 19 to Mar 23, 2020).
- Federal Reserve cut rates to 0–0.25% on March 15, 2020 and launched massive QE.
- Bank of Japan held rates at -0.1% with yield curve control firmly in place. USD/JPY largely range-bound 105–110 through 2020.
- Global economic shutdown; Japan declared its first state of emergency in April 2020.

### Industry context
- Insurance sales channels relying on face-to-face contact were severely disrupted.
- Mortality, hospitalization, and claims experience were all uncertain — life insurers carrying meaningful pandemic risk; health insurers with telemedicine and treatment-shift complexity.
- The asset side of insurance balance sheets was hit by spread-widening, equity declines, and credit downgrades.

### Aflac-specific
- Aflac's full-year 2020 sales: Japan **-38%**, U.S. **-31%**. Devastating absolute declines.
- The in-force block remained stable — premium income held up because lapses didn't spike materially.
- Capital position remained strong throughout, supporting continued dividend.
- Distribution disruption was the dominant operational story: U.S. agents couldn't visit small employers, Japan agents couldn't visit households or branches.

### PR Lens
Management's narrative during 2020 emphasized **resilience of the in-force block** and **brand strength** rather than new business growth. Listen for the consistent emphasis on persistency, capital strength, and the dividend — signals that the company was making the case for stability while sales were collapsing. Avoid reading recovery commentary in 2021–2022 as triumphant; it was rebuilding from a deep trough.

## Inflection 2 — Q4 2021 / Q1 2022 — End of Zero-Rate Era (call: Feb / May 2022)

### Macro backdrop
- U.S. CPI peaked at 9.1% in June 2022; inflation was the dominant macro story.
- Federal Reserve began aggressive hiking: first 25bp hike in March 2022, accelerated to 75bp hikes by mid-2022.
- 10-year U.S. Treasury yield rose from ~1.5% (early 2022) to ~3.5%+ by mid-year.
- Russia invaded Ukraine on Feb 24, 2022 — energy and commodity shock layered on top of inflation.
- Credit spreads widened materially, particularly in lower-rated corporates.

### Industry context
- Insurance investment portfolios saw large unrealized losses on bond holdings (held-to-maturity vs. available-for-sale designations became consequential).
- Higher rates were medium-term positive for insurance economics (better reinvestment yields, lower LDTI liability values), but transition was painful.
- The industry began discussing — and Aflac began executing — capital optimization through internal reinsurance and Bermuda structures.

### Aflac-specific
- Aflac is largely a buy-and-hold investor — duration matched to long liability profile — so unrealized losses were less of an operating concern than for some peers.
- The sales recovery was uneven: Japan flattish, U.S. recovering but still below 2019 levels.
- Sales productivity per agent began climbing in both segments.

### PR Lens
Higher rates were a long-term positive for the company. But headlines through 2022 focused on inflation, rate volatility, and recession fears, so Aflac's measured commentary on portfolio quality and patient management was deliberately counter-cyclical to broader risk-off sentiment.

## Inflection 3 — Q3 2022 — Peak Yen Weakness (call: late October 2022)

### Macro backdrop
- USD/JPY broke 150 in late October 2022 — the weakest yen against the dollar since August 1990.
- BOJ remained committed to yield curve control under Governor Kuroda; refused to follow Fed in tightening.
- Japanese authorities intervened multiple times to support the yen — first in September 2022 (~$20B), again on October 21 (~$30B).
- The Fed had hiked 300bps year-to-date and signaled another 150bps to come.
- Stark divergence between Fed and BOJ policy was the proximate cause of yen weakness.

### Industry context
- For U.S.-listed companies with major Japan exposure, yen translation was a massive headwind to reported dollar earnings.
- Japanese insurers' overseas asset hedging strategies were under scrutiny.
- The "Mrs. Watanabe" retail FX leverage trade and the broader carry trade were getting attention.

### Aflac-specific
- Yen translation was the dominant earnings narrative for the quarter.
- Management consistently directed analysts to **adjusted EPS ex-FX** as the operating-performance metric.
- Aflac's enterprise hedging strategy — including the deliberate decision to hold roughly 65% of corporate debt in yen — was emphasized as protecting economic value.
- The U.S. dollar program inside Japan's portfolio actually benefited from yen weakness on a yen-translated basis.

### PR Lens
This was the period when the gap between **reported earnings** and **underlying performance** was widest. Management's PR challenge was making sure analysts and reporters didn't conflate translation losses with operating problems. Watch for emphatic differentiation of "currency-neutral" vs. reported metrics in this period and through 2023–2024 as the yen continued to weaken.

## Inflection 4 — Q1 2023 — SVB Collapse and the LDTI Effective Date (call: early May 2023)

### Macro backdrop
- Silicon Valley Bank failed on March 10, 2023 — second-largest U.S. bank failure ever.
- Signature Bank closed March 12, 2023; First Republic seized in early May 2023.
- The Fed launched the Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP) to backstop banks holding underwater bond portfolios.
- Federal regulators backstopped uninsured deposits at SVB and Signature in an emergency action.
- Markets shifted from "is the Fed too hawkish" to "is something breaking" within 48 hours.
- 2-year Treasury yield collapsed ~100bps in days as a flight-to-safety bid for short Treasuries collided with rate-cut expectations.

### Industry context
- The crisis spotlighted the question of **unrealized losses on bond portfolios** across financial institutions.
- Insurance companies' large bond books got intense scrutiny, even though the insurance liability structure (long-tenor, sticky) is fundamentally different from a bank's deposit base.
- Commercial real estate concerns emerged contemporaneously: regional banks plus office-loan vintages plus return-to-office uncertainty.

### Aflac-specific (LDTI go-live)
- January 1, 2023 was the effective date for **LDTI (ASU 2018-12)** for SEC filers including Aflac.
- LDTI fundamentally changed accounting for long-duration insurance contracts: annual assumption updates, standardized discount rates, MRBs at fair value, simplified DAC amortization.
- Aflac restated 2021 and 2022 financials. Comparability with pre-2023 results is limited.
- For Aflac, higher rates at the LDTI transition were a tailwind (lower recognized liabilities); equity impact ran through AOCI.
- Q1 2023 was the first quarter reported under the new framework.

### Aflac-specific (banking crisis)
- Aflac had no direct exposure to SVB or Signature.
- Management addressed credit and CRE questions head-on, emphasizing portfolio quality and the long-duration buy-and-hold posture.
- This is the period when CRE impairments and loan portfolio commentary became a recurring quarterly line item in CFO updates.

### PR Lens
Two narrative threads collided in Q1 2023: a fundamental accounting reset (LDTI) plus a financial-system shock (banks). Management's challenge was preventing either story from spilling into a third — that Aflac was somehow at risk. The discipline of **clear separation between operating fundamentals, accounting changes, and macro fears** matters here. PR teams worked hard to keep these stories distinct.

## Inflection 5 — Q4 2023 — BOJ Policy Shift Signals (call: February 2024)

### Macro backdrop
- BOJ under Governor Ueda began signaling end of negative rates and yield curve control.
- 10-year JGB yields rose toward 0.75–1.0% range, having been pinned near zero for years.
- Markets pricing in a BOJ rate hike in early 2024.
- USD/JPY remained weak — 145–150 range — but the trajectory of Japanese rates was changing.
- U.S. Fed had paused at 5.25–5.50%; rate cut expectations building for 2024.

### Industry context
- Japanese life insurers faced both opportunity and risk from rising domestic yields. Higher reinvestment yields are good; mark-to-market hits on existing JGB books are not.
- Japanese regulator (FSA) ESR framework continued evolving.
- For a U.S.-listed company with major Japan operations like Aflac, the calculus was nuanced.

### Aflac-specific
- Aflac's Japan portfolio is structured to benefit from rising domestic yields over time, given the long-duration liability profile.
- Lapse-and-reissue activity on the Japan cancer block continued; management began providing more granular commentary on lapse mix and IRR economics.
- Reinsurance and capital deployment narratives intensified.

### PR Lens
The "Japan is changing" narrative began running in markets but was highly nuanced. Aflac's messaging emphasized that they were **prepared and positioned** for whichever direction Japanese rates moved. This was management positioning, not market reality — the actual rate path in 2024 was unpredictable and management's verbal hedging reflected that.

## Inflection 6 — Q3 2024 — BOJ Rate Hike & Yen Carry Trade Unwind (call: late October 2024)

### Macro backdrop
- BOJ raised its short-term policy rate from 0–0.1% to 0.25% on July 31, 2024 — the first hike of meaningful size in 17 years.
- Combined with weak U.S. labor data on August 2, 2024, this triggered a violent unwinding of the yen carry trade.
- Nikkei 225 dropped ~20% in three days (July 31–August 5, 2024) — worst since 1987.
- USD/JPY collapsed from ~162 (July high) to ~141 (August low) before stabilizing in 145–150 range.
- Global risk assets sold off sharply for several days; volatility spike across asset classes.
- The Fed cut rates 50bp in September 2024.
- USD/JPY traded back toward 150+ by year-end as carry positioning rebuilt.

### Industry context
- Japanese insurers' investment portfolios — particularly USD-denominated holdings — saw mark-to-market volatility from the FX whipsaw.
- The episode highlighted the systemic importance of yen funding to global risk asset positioning.
- Insurance investment officers re-evaluated hedge ratios and duration matching.

### Aflac-specific
- Aflac's enterprise hedging program is designed for exactly this kind of volatility.
- Reported earnings benefited modestly from yen strength when it occurred, with corresponding mechanical reversals when yen weakened again.
- Underlying operating commentary emphasized stability through volatility.

### PR Lens
The August 2024 episode was a stress test of management's "we hedge for these moves" narrative. Aflac came through it without operational disruption, which became a quiet credibility win that shows up in subsequent quarters' tone. The contrast with companies caught off-guard by the FX swings was unspoken but real.

## Inflection 7 — Q2 2025 — Aflac Cybersecurity Incident (call: early August 2025)

### Macro backdrop
- A coordinated wave of cybersecurity attacks on the U.S. insurance sector during summer 2025, attributed by federal law enforcement and outside experts to a sophisticated threat actor (publicly speculated to be Scattered Spider).
- Multiple insurance carriers disclosed breaches in close succession.
- Macro conditions otherwise stable; markets pricing in further Fed easing.

### Aflac-specific event timeline
- **June 12, 2025** — Aflac identified suspicious activity on its U.S. network. Initiated incident response protocols and contained the intrusion within hours.
- **June 20, 2025** — Aflac filed an 8-K with the SEC and issued a public press release disclosing the incident. Characterized it as part of a "cybercrime campaign" against the insurance industry.
- The threat actor reportedly used social engineering to gain access. Aflac's systems were not affected by ransomware; business operations remained continuous.
- Sensitive data was exfiltrated, including claims information, health information, Social Security numbers, and other personal information.
- **December 4, 2025** — Aflac determined the impacted files likely contained personal information.
- **December 19, 2025** — Aflac issued a public update.
- The U.S. Senate demanded details on the breach.
- The 2025 10-K disclosed personal information of approximately **22.65 million individuals** was involved.

### Industry/regulatory context
